The Book

What This Is

In 1986, my parents introduced me to God and Santa Claus with the same voice and the same certainty. When Santa fell, God fell with him.

What followed was thirty years — through my parents' divorce, a childhood built on nothing, addiction, prison, and a bunk in Hutchins State Jail where a copy of Michael Behe's Darwin's Black Box landed in my hands and something buried since childhood began to surface.

I spent seven years convinced life was designed but unconvinced the designer cared. I found recovery through Narcotics Anonymous — went back, came back, went back again, until I finally got it. Really got it. I spent years as a follower of God who refused to call himself a Christian — teaching the science of design at a small church on Wednesday nights while leaving the resurrection alone because I had not verified it.

Then I emailed Dr. James Tour of Rice University — one of the most cited chemists in the world — expecting him not to respond. He called me back. On December 18, 2021, I gave my life to Christ. Not because someone told me to. Because I ran out of reasons not to.

Under Every Rock Turned is the complete manuscript. 55,000 words. Final form, ready for editing and publication. It is not a devotional. It is not a self-help book. It is a case file — built from irreducible complexity, fine-tuning constants, the consciousness problem, the historical resurrection evidence, and a geometric framework for the origin of evil that I have not found in any other book on the shelf.

No book in the Christian apologetics canon combines addiction, prison, scientific argument, and a genuine atheist-to-Christian conversion told in a street-level voice. This one does.

Sample Chapters

Three chapters. The hook, the first rock, and the climax. If you want the rest, the manuscript is ready.

In 1986, Halley's Comet returned to the inner solar system for the first time in seventy-six years. It had been seen by the Babylonians, recorded in the Bayeux Tapestry, watched by Mark Twain on the night he was born and the night he died. It had been circling the sun since before anyone thought to write anything down. It was visible from Earth because the universe it traveled through was ordered to a precision that staggers the imagination — a precision I would not learn about for another twenty years, but that was already holding every atom in place while a four-year-old looked up.

I was four years old. I did not know any of that. What I knew was that it was nighttime, and I was outside, and my whole family was with me. We were standing in the backyard of our house in Grand Prairie, Texas, looking south toward the horizon. Someone pointed, and I looked where they pointed, and I saw — something. A smudge. A blur. A faint mark on the sky that the adults around me seemed to think mattered. I have almost no memories from that age. But I have this one. Not the comet itself — I barely registered what it was. What I remember is the feeling. The night air. The adults quiet for once, all looking at the same piece of sky. The sense that something was happening that was bigger than me, bigger than our yard, bigger than anything I had a word for.

I was conscious. I did not have that word yet, but that is what I was. A small, breathing, aware thing, standing on a rock hurtling through space at sixty-seven thousand miles an hour, watching a chunk of ice that had been falling around the same star since before Rome or Egypt or language itself. And I felt it. Not understood it. Felt it. The weight of something vast and ancient, pressing against the edges of a four-year-old's awareness.

I felt it because I was built to feel it.

I did not know that yet. It would take me a lifetime to understand what that feeling was. A lifetime of contemplation, of philosophy turning over inside my head, of conversations with people over a cigarette, of quiet nights lying still with my eyes wide open staring straight up — deep and thankful and serene, listening for voices from the dark abyss staring back at me.

Nietzsche said that if you gaze long into an abyss, the abyss gazes also into you. He meant it as a warning. I lived it as a hope. For years I stood at the edge of that darkness, staring in, wishing something was staring back. Hoping. But hopeless.

Recognition happens slowly over years, like seeing the faint outline of barely a shape in the darkness, not knowing what it is until the light begins to illuminate it — slightly more, only ever slightly more, revealing itself at certain pivotal, paradigm-shifting points. I wanted to know what it was. Not with the vocabulary I would have later — not cosmology, not consciousness, not God — but with the wordless, wide-open want of a child who has just been shown that the universe is enormous and has not yet been told it is empty. Every step I took toward it was just the act of remembering what I already knew was there in the darkness, just beyond sight.

This is the story of those pivotal lessons. And how I found the words to describe what was there all along.

In my early twenties I was sitting on a bunk in Hutchins State Jail, dealing with the accumulated wreckage of those rough teenage years. Hutchins was a seventy-five man cell. Open showers. Open toilets. The smell was permanent — sweat and disinfectant and something underneath both that you never got used to.

Someone gave me a book. It was called Darwin's Black Box, by Michael Behe. I had not asked for it. I do not remember who handed it to me. But I read it.

And the room went quiet. Not actually quiet — the noise never stopped in Hutchins. But somewhere between the second and third chapter, I stopped hearing any of it. There was only the page in front of me, and a voice rising off of it that did not sound like religion. It sounded like engineering.

Behe was drawing diagrams in my head. Machines. Structures so small no human eye could see them, but so precise they looked engineered — spinning, interlocking, alive. I could see them — not on the page, but on the walls of the cell, projected there by something in my mind that had been waiting for this information the way a lock waits for a key.

Behe's argument was about what he called irreducible complexity. He examined the molecular machinery inside living cells — structures like the bacterial flagellum, a literal rotary motor made of protein, complete with a stator, rotor, drive shaft, and universal joint — and asked a question that I had never heard anyone ask before: could this have been built one piece at a time by an undirected process? His answer was no. And he explained why with a precision that did not feel like religion. It felt like engineering.

The argument is straightforward. Some biological systems require multiple interdependent parts to function at all. Remove any single component and you do not get a degraded version of the system — you get nothing. No function whatsoever. A mousetrap with four out of five parts does not catch mice poorly. It does not catch mice at all. And natural selection — the engine that supposedly built all of life's complexity — can only preserve things that already work. It cannot select for a system that has no function until all its parts are present simultaneously.

The bacterial flagellum has approximately forty protein components. It spins at up to seventeen thousand RPM. It can reverse direction in a quarter turn. And every one of those components is necessary for the motor to function at all. This is not a simple structure that got incrementally better over time. This is a system that either works completely or does not work at all.

And the alternative, followed to its logical end? The materialist explanation for the origin of life, when you trace it all the way back — past the amino acids, past the minerals, past the water — is that a rock got wet. The consensus-science explanation for your existence is that a rock got wet and eventually wrote Shakespeare. A mousetrap says otherwise.

Life was designed. And here is what I need you to understand about where this left me: intelligent design proves a designer. It does not prove God. The flagellum could have been built by aliens. The cell could be the product of a simulation. Irreducible complexity gets you to this was intentional. It does not get you to this was love.

I left state jail convinced that life was designed. I was not convinced it was designed by someone who gives a damn about me. The seed was planted. But a seed is not a tree. And for seven more years, I would water it with nothing.

Dr. James Tour. Rice University. One of the most cited chemists in the world — over eight hundred publications, over a hundred and thirty patents. A man who built molecules for a living, who understood the chemistry of life at a level I never would and never pretended to. And in the videos I watched, he did something I had never seen a scientist do: he dismantled abiogenesis — the claim that life arose from non-living chemistry — without saying a single word about God. He stood in front of audiences and said: show me the mechanism. Show me how you get from simple molecules to a living cell. Show me the pathway. And when they could not — when no one could, because no one has — he said so, plainly, the way a scientist says so when the data does not support the conclusion.

Then I saw the video where he said: if you do not believe this, text me. It shocked me. Not the content — the invitation. Because suddenly the top guy, the one I respected precisely because he was not a sensationalist, was making a claim I did not expect. He was not just presenting evidence. He was offering himself. Personally. To strangers.

I emailed him. I did not email him looking for God. I emailed him looking for a crack in his credibility. I wanted to catch him not meaning it. I wanted the silence that would confirm what I already believed — that the invitation was performative, that even the best of them were selling something, that no one at his level would actually respond to a stranger on the internet.

He called me back.

Not an assistant. Not a form letter. James Tour, on the phone, talking to me. And the first thing he did was tell me his story. November 7, 1977. Room 1812, Barnes Dormitory. He was a young man with a pornography addiction that had started at age fourteen. He had met a football player in the laundry room, a kid doing his first load of laundry because his mother had always done it. That football player invited him to a Bible study. And in room 1812, Tour gave his life to Christ. He told me this — all of it — without flinching. A man with eight hundred publications and the respect of the global chemistry community told a stranger about his pornography addiction and the night Jesus showed up in a dormitory room.

Because he was showing me something: this religion is for sinners.

Then he asked me about William. He used my son's name. He said: what would you do, Jon, if William was in the yard and a big dog came running at him? I said I would put myself between William and the dog. "That is what God did," Tour said. "He demonstrates his love toward us. While we were still sinners, Christ died for us."

Then he walked me through the evidence I had been avoiding for thirteen years. Twelve extrabiblical sources for Jesus of Nazareth. The crucifixion performed by Roman soldiers — professionals who knew how to kill a man and whose careers depended on doing it correctly. Thomas, who would not believe until his hand was in the wound — a skeptic, like me. Five hundred witnesses to the resurrection, reported by Paul within years of the event, when most of those witnesses were still alive to be questioned.

And then Tour told me about Tacitus, and something inside me broke.

Tacitus was a Roman senator and historian — not a Christian, not a sympathizer, not a man with any reason to exaggerate. He was writing the official history of Rome. And he recorded that Nero, after the great fire of 64 AD, blamed the Christians and subjected them to what Tacitus called the most exquisite tortures. He described a vast multitude of people who were arrested, convicted, and executed — not for setting the fire, but for what they claimed to have seen.

A vast multitude. Not twelve. Not a handful of fanatics. A vast multitude. And here is what hit me: some of those people were related to each other. Families. Parents and children. Brothers and sisters. Which means some of them watched their relatives tortured to death in front of them — covered in animal skins and torn apart by dogs, crucified, set on fire as human torches to light Nero's gardens — and then faced the same choice themselves.

And the choice was simple. All they had to do — the only thing required to stop the torture, to save themselves, to save their children — was say they had not seen Jesus alive after his death. That is all. And not only would the torture stop — according to Tacitus, they would have been rewarded. All they had to do was recant. Not one of them did.

Not one. A vast multitude of people, watching each other die in the most imaginative and creative ways that the Roman Empire could devise — and every single one of them chose the fire. Parents watched their children die and still would not say the words that would have saved them.

Because saying those words would have been a lie, and they knew it was a lie, because they had seen what they had seen. People do not die for lies they know are lies.

"And you know how I know the truth is already on your heart, Jon?" "Because I emailed you," I said.

"That's right." I broke.

He asked me to pray with him. I was standing in my house. It was December 18, 2021. I had fought this for over twenty years. I had turned every rock I could find, and under every single one of them the same face had been waiting, and I had spent two decades telling myself it was not there.

The war was over. The truth I had been circling for twenty years had finally caught me, and I had finally stopped running, and the only feeling in the room was the feeling of a man who has been holding his breath for two decades and has finally, finally exhaled.
